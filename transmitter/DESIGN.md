Single-ACK semantics on broadcast
The CAN ACK slot is a single dominant bit driven by any node that correctly received the frame. The transmitter sends recessive during this slot and only observes whether the bus was pulled dominant — it cannot distinguish "one node ACKed" from "all nodes ACKed". CAN itself does not provide per-receiver delivery confirmation; a frame is considered transmitted successfully as long as at least one node on the bus acknowledged it. Detecting a receiver that has stopped listening is an application-layer concern on CAN (heartbeats, watchdog frames, presence counters), not a transport-layer guarantee.

WCAN preserves this semantic exactly. The sender broadcasts the frame, and the first ACK from any peer ends the retry loop — see wcan_sender.cpp:61-76 and wcan_sender.cpp:228-232. Requiring N ACKs from N expected receivers would be stronger than CAN, not equivalent: it would force the sender to maintain a registry of peers, which CAN deliberately avoids in order to support hot-plug nodes and write-only ECUs. Choosing first-ACK-wins is therefore the design that matches CAN, not the design that diverges from it.

Batching at high sample rates
ESP-NOW is a packet-radio link, not a bus. Each transmission carries fixed protocol overhead (MAC header, link-layer ACK round-trip, optional encryption) and the radio cannot sustain the per-frame rate a wired CAN bus handles natively. Sending a 100 Hz signal as 100 individual ESP-NOW packets per second consumes a disproportionate share of the airtime budget, especially when multiple CAN IDs share the medium. There were two reasonable responses to this:

Per-receiver unicast — would let us tune retries per peer and bypass the airtime issue, but requires the sender to know its receivers in advance, breaking the register-once-and-listen behaviour that makes CAN convenient in a vehicle harness.
Broadcast with bounded batching — keeps the bus-like topology (any node may subscribe to any ID without sender-side registration) at the cost of grouping high-rate samples into a single over-the-air packet.
WCAN takes route (2). Preserving CAN's broadcast model was prioritised over preserving 1:1 frame timing for high-rate signals. Batching is gated by linger_ms in wcan_sender.cpp:25-58: after the first sample on a CAN ID arrives, the sender waits up to linger_ms for additional samples before transmitting. Signals whose inter-sample gap is larger than linger_ms are sent the moment the first sample arrives and therefore behave as if no batching existed — they are effectively real-time. Only signals fast enough to produce multiple samples within one linger window are coalesced, and only because the alternative is dropping samples or saturating the radio.

This is a deliberate trade-off, not a transparency loss. The batch carries one tick_count corresponding to the moment of transmission, which is sufficient for telemetry-style consumers that ingest samples in order at their own rate. If a future use case needs sub-batch timing fidelity (e.g. control loops driven off the wireless link, which is itself outside WCAN's scope), per-sample timestamps could be added inside the payload without changing the protocol envelope.
